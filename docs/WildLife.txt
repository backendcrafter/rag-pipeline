Wildlife refers to all living organisms that exist in their natural habitats without being domesticated by humans. This includes mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, fish, insects, and plants.

Forests cover about 31% of the Earth's land area and are home to more than 80% of terrestrial species. The Amazon rainforest alone contains about 10% of all species on Earth.

Tigers are one of the most endangered big cats, with fewer than 4,000 remaining in the wild. They are found primarily in South and Southeast Asia. India has the largest tiger population with around 3,000 tigers.

African elephants are the largest land animals on Earth. They can weigh up to 7 tons and live for 60-70 years. Elephants are keystone species — their presence shapes entire ecosystems.

Migration is one of nature's most remarkable phenomena. The Arctic Tern migrates from the Arctic to the Antarctic and back every year — a round trip of about 70,000 kilometers.

Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor but support 25% of all marine species. They are often called the rainforests of the sea. Climate change and ocean acidification are their biggest threats.

Wolves are apex predators that play a crucial role in maintaining ecological balance. When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park in 1995, they triggered a trophic cascade that transformed the entire ecosystem.

Deforestation destroys about 15 billion trees per year. This destroys habitats for countless species and contributes significantly to climate change. Palm oil plantations are a major driver of deforestation in Southeast Asia.

Biodiversity hotspots are regions with exceptionally high species diversity that are under significant threat. There are 36 recognized hotspots worldwide, covering only 2.5% of Earth's land but containing more than half of all plant species.

Poaching remains one of the biggest threats to wildlife. Rhinos are killed for their horns, elephants for ivory, and pangolins for their scales. Pangolins are the most trafficked mammals in the world.
